Museum Asia Afrika


The KAA Museum, where Asia-Africa history is hoisted, where the Asia Africa Partnership is established for a better future. Located in Bandung, the Museum of KAA has a history as a place of the Asian-African Conference of 1955.

Museum of the Asian-African Conference is one of the museums located in the city of Bandung located at Jalan Asia Afrika No. 65. The museum is a memorabilia of the Asian-African Conference. This museum has a very close relationship with Gedung Merdeka. Overall Gedung Merdeka has two main buildings, the first is called Gedung Merdeka as the main meeting place, while next to Gedung Merdeka is Museum of Asian-African Conference as a memorabilia of Asian-African Conference. The background of the construction of this museum is the desire of the leaders of the nations in Asia and Africa to know about Gedung Merdeka and surrounding areas where the Asian-African Conference took place. This makes the Minister of Foreign Affairs of the Republic of Indonesia, Prof. Dr. Mochtar Kusumaatmadja, S.H., LL.M has an idea to build a museum. The idea was presented at the forum of the 25th Anniversary Committee of the Asian-African Conference (1980) attended by Director General of Culture Prof. Dr. Haryati Soebadio as the representative of the Ministry of Education and Culture. Then the museum was inaugurated on April 24, 1980 to coincide with the 25th anniversary of the Asian-African Conference.

Saung Angklung Udjo


Saung Angklung Udjo (SAU) is a place that is a place of performances, a center of bamboo handicrafts, and workshop of musical instruments from bamboo. In addition, SAU has a goal as an educational laboratory and learning center to maintain Sundanese culture and especially angklung. Established in 1966 by Udjo Ngalagena and his wife Uum Sumiati, with a view to preserving and maintaining traditional Sundanese art and culture. Located at Jalan Padasuka 118, Bandung Timur West Java Indonesia.

With fresh air atmosphere typical of parahyangan city, make SAU as the right place to enjoy traditional art from bamboo, ranging from saung-saung and interior made of bamboo and strains of tone of bamboo musical instrument. Thus making nature and culture an expensive element to be gained. This makes SAU as a complete cultural tourism place to be presented to the tourists who want to enjoy Sundanese culture as a world cultural heritage.

In addition to routine performances every afternoon, Saung Angklung Udjo has held numerous special shows performed in the morning or afternoon. The performances were not limited to the location of Saung Angklung Udjo only, but various invitations performed at various places both at home and abroad, in August 2000 at Sasana Budaya Ganesha ITB, Bandung, Saung Angklung Udjo held a collaboration concert with a young singer who nicknamed Shirley Temple of Indonesia, Sherina.

China Town


The Chinese first came to Indonesia through the expedition of Haj Admiral Muhammad Cheng Hoo (1405-1433). At that time, Cheng Hoo traveled the world to open silk and ceramic lines. Cheng Hoo had once set foot on the island of Java. Since the expedition, gradually the Chinese came and built Chinatowns in several areas of Java. Kuncen Bandung, Haryoto Kunto in Face Bandoeng Tempo Doeloe (Granesia, 1984), said that some Chinese citizens in Java moved to Bandung during Diponegoro war (1825). Arriving in Bandung, most live in the village of Suniaraja and around Old Chinatown Street. They settled and made a living there. In 1885 they began to spread to Jln. Temple. Chinatown on Jln. The temple is marked with the construction of the Vihara Satya Budhi. According to the Vihara Satya Budhi administrators, Chinatowns in Bandung are like shop houses in general, there are no special accessories such as pecinan in other regions in Indonesia. Its citizens also vary, not only Chinese descent. Chinatown has grown rapidly around the New Market since 1905. Generally Tionghoa people become merchants.

Tan Sioe How who founded the "Babah Kuya" herbal stall in Jln. Behind the Market, 1910 was one of the pioneering shops in the region. The Chinese culture, Drs. Soeria Disastra, said that Chinans exist, but there is no limit. That is, the relations between the Chinese and the Natives around the nineteenth century are very close. However, the Dutch were not happy to see the closeness. The Dutch also separated the Chinese and the Natives from an economic point of view. Chinese citizens made intermediaries of the economy of Europeans and natives. Sell ​​spices from indigenous to Dutch for export. Over time the proximity also faded. In addition to Pasar Baru, Chinatown also grew at Suniaraja and Citepus in 1914. Each Chinatown was led by Wijkmeester. Wijkmeester for Suniaraja area is Thung Pek Koey, while for citepus Tan Nyim Coy area. Wijkmeester is led by a Luitennant der Chineeschen. In Bandung, Luitennant was Tan Djoen Liong (H. Buning, "Maleische Almanak", 1914). The Chinese leaders were immortalized in several places such as around the Chinees-Wijk Citepus road, there were also Gang Goan Ann in Andir and Jap Lun. Sie Tjoe Liong explained, when the Bandung Fire of the Ocean (1946). New Market Stalls burned by Dutch soldiers. Bandung area apart into the north and south. Both areas are restricted to long railroad rail from Cimahi to Kiara Condong. The northern regions were occupied by the Dutch, while the south by the natives and foreigners. As a result of the incident, Chinese people fled to Tegalega, Kosambi, Sudirman, and Cimindi areas. Thus, from Pasar Baru, Chinatown territory extends to these areas. The Chinese and the natives were reunited. The Netherlands calls this area "Groote Post Weg".
